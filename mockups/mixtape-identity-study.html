My read.

A is fine but forgettable. Your eye doesn't register the stripe because it reads as system chrome.
B makes the stripe meaningful — it "seals" the user's identity badge. But it's tied to the badge, so it won't appear on screens without a tracker badge (Following, All). That's either a feature (stripe = "this is your page") or a bug (inconsistent brand gesture).
C elevates the bottom nav. The nav becomes a proper object, not a flat bar. Shows on every screen. Feels intentional.
D is the most honest answer — some desktop gestures simply don't miniaturize, and pretending otherwise is forcing the pattern. Mobile carries the brand through other means.

If forced to pick: C or D. C if you want the brand gesture to persist across all screens; D if you want mobile to have its own integrity.

Wordmark · which part is red
a small but meaningful brand decision
The problem. The live app uses showup. — "up" in red. The Mixtape mockups have been using showup. — the period in red. These read differently. Pick deliberately.

"up" is the verb. Showing up is the whole point of the app, so coloring the verb is on-brand. Reads as a two-word phrase: show + up. The period becomes a quiet period, not a statement.

My honest read.

A feels warm and literal. You see "show up" and you get it immediately. On very small sizes the colored "up" can get lost though (at 0.9rem the two letters become visual noise). It's the "friendly habit tracker" logo.

B feels more like a brand, less like a product name. The period as red mark is iconic — you could see just the red "." and recognize showup. That's a durable design move. The meaning shifts: instead of "show up is the action," the period becomes "a full stop. showing up. period." — which actually echoes the tagline on the login screen ("Show up. Period.") nicely.

My lean: B. It gives you a mark that can stand alone (section 3), it scales better to tiny sizes (the period is visible even at 0.7rem), and it resonates with the "Show up. Period." tagline you already have. But A is the sentimental choice and has history on your side.
03
Standalone mark · when the wordmark doesn't fit
app icon, avatar, favicon, social profile
The problem. App icons are 60×60 squares. Favicons are 16×16. Circle avatars on third-party platforms (Twitter, LinkedIn) force a round crop. The full wordmark is useless in all these. You need a mark. Four candidates below, all built from showup.'s existing language — no new shapes invented.
M1 · The period just the dot
App icon
Avatar
Favicon
The most minimal. Just the red period, floated in a square/circle. Works only if people already associate a red dot with showup. — which means this is a "brand-has-permission" mark, not a "introduces-brand" mark. High risk if people don't know you yet.
M2 · s. initialism + period
s.
s.
App icon
s.
Avatar
s.
Favicon
Initialism + period. Uses the same Sora 800 + red period system as the wordmark. Legible at small sizes. Scales to any square. The "s" is quietly unclaimed — Signal uses it, but at different weights and colors. Safe and consistent.
M3 · The sparkle from the sidebar motif
App icon
Avatar
Favicon
The motif from above the desktop logo. A small sparkle + yellow dot + blue squiggle. Already part of the brand system. But at favicon scale (16px) it becomes illegible — too much detail. Better as a social avatar or app icon than favicon.
M4 · The ribbon the stripe alone
App icon
Avatar
Favicon
Three stripes stacked. Takes the ribbon-around-the-book idea and turns it into the entire mark. Reads as "Mixtape" visually (like the three sides of a cassette label). Most distinctive at all sizes. Risk: a little reminiscent of Microsoft / Windows flag logos if you squint.
My read.

M1 (just the period) is only powerful if you already know the brand. New users see "a red circle," not "showup." Deferred — not ready for this stage of the product.

M2 (s.) is the safest. Uses your existing typography + color language. Scales to any size. The one VSCii can implement in 10 minutes. Feels most "showup." without trying.

M3 (sparkle) is cute but detailed. Works at app-icon scale, breaks at favicon scale. Could be a secondary mark for social avatars where you have room to breathe.

M4 (ribbon) is the most distinctive. If the Mixtape aesthetic is really the direction, this mark leans all the way into it. Risk: tell that Microsoft comparison to go away the moment you see it next to "showup." anywhere.

My pick, ranked: M2 primary (daily use, app icon, favicon), M3 secondary (social avatars, marketing). M4 is worth keeping in your back pocket. M1 comes back once showup. has brand recognition.
NOTES
What to do with these decisions
next steps are clear
The three decisions — stripe placement, wordmark variant, standalone mark — all go into MIXTAPE_SPEC.md under a "Brand identity" section. Once you pick each, I lock them as tokens (for the stripe), typography rules (for the wordmark), and SVG assets (for the mark).

On implementation: the wordmark is a trivial CSS swap. The stripe is already in the mockups, just needs a position decision. The mark becomes an SVG in assets/icons/ — VSCii can ship it in the same PR as the favicon and app icon updates.

Next, after these three are locked: we finalize MIXTAPE_SPEC.md and then implementation begins. Everything else (Rockstar theme, refining motifs, etc.) becomes post-ship work that the spec supports rather than blocks.
