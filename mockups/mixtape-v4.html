D
Cassette insert (vertical strip)
narrow strip, labels rotated, track numbers above each
What this is. A narrow vertical strip, 72px wide. Nav items are rotated labels reading bottom-to-top like the spine of a book or the vertical label on a cassette insert. Tiny track numbers sit above each label. Active item has a red left-bar and italic. Logo also rotated. Very distinctive, high visual commitment. Risk: rotated text is slightly harder to scan quickly, and this is literally the cassette metaphor we agreed to soften. Included because you asked.

E
Edge rail (ccunpacked-style)
tiny floating pill column · hover reveals labels
What this is. A tiny floating pill column on the left edge, frosted and translucent. Each nav item is a 32px circle showing just the number. Hover any item to see its label appear to the right on a dark pill. Active item is filled red. Logo floats top-left of the main content area. User chip and theme pill float bottom-left in a matching frosted pill. Feels like a scroll indicator or a document index, not a nav bar.

F
Top tabs, restyled (typographic)
no sidebar · nav is a typographic row at the top
What this is. Kill the sidebar entirely. Nav lives as a row of words across the top, set in Fraunces, separated by generous space. Active item is italic with a red underline. Logo sits top-left with a small italic tagline next to it. User chip and theme pill sit top-right. This is the most "magazine page" option. Note: this is what we moved away from earlier (Claude-like top bar), but done as typography instead of pills, which changes the feel completely. Check if it still reads as too-app-like or if the editorial treatment makes it feel right.

G
Hover-expand rail
narrow by default · hover the sidebar to see labels
What this is. Sidebar is only 64px wide by default, showing just dots. Hover the sidebar area and it smoothly expands to 240px revealing full labels. Very VS-Code-activity-bar, but slowed down and paper-textured instead of dark. Risk: the discovery moment ("wait, I hover it?") can feel clever in a distracting way. Also, screens with touch input can't hover, so this needs a different mobile pattern anyway.

Verdict time
scroll back up, hover around, pick one
A still the reference. The left-margin feeling.

D cassette insert. Narrow, rotated labels. Most distinctive, most literal.

E edge rail. Floating, frosted, hover-reveal. Most editorial, most "object inside a page." Try the hover, it's fun.

F top tabs. No sidebar at all. Most magazine-layout. Gives the content the most horizontal space.

G hover-expand. Narrow at rest, wide on hover. Most space-efficient, most clever-risk.

Tell me which wins, or which two you want me to hybridize. Remember: we need to lock this soon so we can move to implementation planning.
